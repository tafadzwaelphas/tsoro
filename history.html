Tsoro

History, meaning & rules of the Shona 4-row mancala

▶ Play Home

What does "Tsoro" mean?

Tsoro is a generic Shona word for board games, not the name of one specific game. Different variants are traditionally distinguished by adding a qualifier — for example Tsoro Yematatu ("tsoro of three") names an entirely different game: a triangular, three-piece alignment game with no captures, closer in spirit to Nine Men's Morris than to mancala. This project is the 4-row sowing variant — the one usually just called Tsoro — where players sow and capture seeds around two rows of pits.

(Earlier versions of this project mislabeled it "Tsoro Yematatu" — that's been corrected.)

History & cultural significance

Tsoro is an ancient strategy game with roots in Zimbabwe, said to have been played for over a thousand years. It belongs to the same family of African sowing games as Oware, Bao, and Omweso — collectively known as mancala.

"Tsoro was played by warriors to improve their enemy capturing and raiding strategies in war situations" and "was also used to teach young boys and girls how to count." "Kings and chiefs often settled disputes by playing Tsoro."

In its traditional form, the game didn't need a manufactured board at all — rows of small holes were dug directly into the ground and populated with pebbles or seeds. The 4-row, 7-holes-per-row layout used here is the most common modern configuration.

How to play

Each player owns the two rows nearest them — an outer (back) row and an inner (front) row — forming a closed loop of 14 pits they sow around.

  • Setup. Every pit starts with 2 seeds. Player 1 takes the bottom two rows, Player 2 the top two.
  • Sow. Pick any of your own pits holding ≥2 seeds, empty it, and drop one seed into each following pit around your two-row loop.
  • Relay. If your last seed lands in a non-empty pit on your outer row, pick up everything there and keep sowing.
  • Capture. If your last seed lands in your inner row and the opponent's pits directly opposite (same column, their two rows) hold any seeds, capture all of them and continue sowing with the captured seeds.
  • Turn ends when your last seed lands on an empty outer pit, or an inner pit with nothing to capture.
  • Win. The game ends when a player has no seeds left, or no legal move — their opponent wins.

The play page has interactive worked examples (Sowing / Capture / Relay) if you'd rather learn by watching a move play out.
